Maximising the value of information

Businesses face myriad issues, many of which are often out of their control. From taxes to a shrinking working-age population, supply chain issues to geopolitical problems, companies must be flexible enough to respond quickly . This is where digital transformation can make the difference. The ability to review business processes and improve operations according to rapidly changing market conditions requires the application of technology to processes, products, and operations.  Digital transformation (DX) is not a passing phase. It is essential for achieving sustainable growth. Accenture found that companies that promote and engage in DX have five times higher revenue growth rates than those that don't.

Business growth through DX

Successful digital transformation of business processes happens in two stages: identifying what problems exist and establishing the precise information needed to solve those problems. When information is stored in different unstructured and scattered forms, this can be an obstacle to true transformation. By smoothing out the flow of information, barriers between departments can disappear, creating a cooperative environment where employees will flourish doing more creative work. Ultimately, improving the quality of a company's operations can create new opportunities resulting in growth.

Having a strategy to deal with the information oversupply is essential for companies to survive and thrive in a competitive business world. With technology innovating so rapidly and market conditions constantly evolving, the challenges faced by companies in APAC are endless. In order to respond to these changes, companies must understand the value of their information and turn it into a new tool that will enhance their competitive advantage.

Understanding how to increase the value of information can be tricky. As information takes a wide range of forms. At its broadest, documents can either be in digital or paper form. At a more granular level, though, information can include the number of emails received or hours spent in meetings, all of which can be used to encourage productivity by identifying workflow bottlenecks. While the amount of information being handled by companies is increasing year-on-year, the vast majority is not ordered effectively.
